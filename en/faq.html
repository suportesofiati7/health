How to read this page

This FAQ answers real questions, not just booking details.

Every answer starts from a simple idea: advanced aesthetics should combine the right indication, honest conversation, safe technique and well-aligned expectations. When the best answer is to wait, prepare the skin or choose another path, that is also part of care.

Care and approach

How Franciele thinks about care

Questions about listening, indication, boundaries and public-facing care.

01What is the first conversation like?

The first conversation is designed to understand you before discussing a procedure. She looks at your concern, history, routine, skin, expectations, hesitations and what you have already tried. That makes the recommendation more responsible and less automatic.

02Does she recommend a procedure right away?

Not always. Sometimes the best first step is to calm the skin, adjust home care, wait for inflammation to settle or explain why a device is not right for that moment. Recommending less can also be a technical decision.

03Will I feel pressured to do something?

The goal is the opposite. The appointment should make you feel safer to decide, including saying no, asking for time or bringing more questions. A good aesthetic plan has to respect boundaries, budget, routine and personal timing.

04How does she handle very high expectations?

With clarity. She explains what can improve, what is gradual, what depends on individual response and what should not be promised. Natural-looking, safe work matters more than promising a fast transformation.

05Can I come in without knowing what treatment I want?

Yes. Many people only know what bothers them: pigmentation, texture, laxity, acne, unwanted hair, small vessels or hair thinning. The consultation helps translate that concern into realistic options and priorities.

Services and devices

What can be treated

A practical view of services and when each path may make sense.

06What kinds of treatments does she offer?

Care may involve technologies and procedures for skin quality, laser, texture, pigmentation, facial vessels, rejuvenation, laxity, unwanted hair, scalp concerns and overall skin health. The indication depends on assessment, not only on the treatment name.

07Can laser help pigmentation, vessels and texture?

It can, but laser is not one single answer. The type of pigmentation, skin tone, sun history, sensitivity, medication and pigmentation risk all affect the handpiece, settings, interval and preparation.

08Does laser hair removal work for everyone?

It works best when there is contrast between hair and skin and when the hair has enough pigment. Very light, white or red hair may respond less. Hormones, treatment area and consistency also matter.

09Are Ultraformer, radiofrequency and collagen stimulation for laxity?

They may help firmness, contour and collagen stimulation, but they do not replace surgery when there is significant excess skin. The right indication depends on laxity, skin thickness and the goal.

10Are peels and CO2 laser similar?

Both can renew texture and skin quality, but they work differently and require different recovery. CO2 usually has a more visible downtime; peels vary widely in depth and purpose.

Skin, acne and pigmentation

When skin needs a strategy

Answers for people who want improvement without damaging the skin barrier.

11My skin is sensitive. Can I still have treatment?

It may be possible, but sensitive skin asks for more preparation and less rushing. Sometimes the priority is repairing the barrier, reducing irritation and simplifying home care before laser, peeling or stronger procedures.

12Can pigmentation get worse if treated incorrectly?

Yes. Melasma and hyperpigmentation can react poorly to heat, sun, inflammation or overly aggressive protocols. That is why planning often includes sun protection, preparation, careful device choice and follow-up.

13Can acne scars improve in one session?

Usually not. Scars often need a staged plan with technologies, intervals and time for remodelling. Improvement can be visible, but it is usually progressive and variable.

14Does professional skin cleansing still matter?

Yes, when it is well indicated. It can prepare the skin, reduce buildup, improve comfort and support other treatments. The point is not to treat cleansing as a universal answer for everything.

15Can active acne be treated with procedures?

It depends on the type and intensity of acne. In some cases inflammation should be controlled first; in others, procedures may support the plan. Persistent or severe acne may also need medical follow-up.

Preparation and recovery

What to expect before and after

General guidance for arriving prepared and respecting recovery.

16Do I need to prepare my skin first?

Often, yes. Preparation may mean avoiding sun, pausing irritating actives, improving hydration, calming inflammation or adjusting habits. Good preparation reduces risk and improves predictability.

17Can I go back to work the same day?

It depends on the procedure. Some have subtle recovery; others can leave redness, swelling, crusting or sensitivity for a few days. Guidance should match your work and social routine.

18Why is avoiding sun so important?

Sun increases the risk of pigmentation, irritation and uneven recovery, especially after laser, peels and procedures that intentionally create controlled inflammation. Sun protection is not a detail; it is part of the result.

19What if I have a reaction afterwards?

You should contact the advised channel, avoid manipulating the area, avoid self-medicating and avoid random advice. Depending on the situation, she may request photos, ask you to return or refer you for medical assessment.

20When will I see results?

Some effects appear quickly, like glow or gradual hair reduction through sessions. Others depend on collagen, healing and remodelling, which take weeks or months. A good result is not always an immediate result.

Public communication

Communication, privacy and trust

How questions, images, limits and follow-up are handled.

21Can I ask many questions?

Yes. Questions reveal expectations, fears, routine and priorities. A well-informed person usually follows care better and understands results more realistically.

22Does she post everyone’s before and after photos?

No. Image use is sensitive and depends on authorisation. Even when authorised, it should respect privacy, context and the agreed level of identification.

23How are insecure clients handled?

Insecurity should not be brushed aside. The conversation needs to separate normal fear, technical doubt, unrealistic expectation and outside pressure. Sometimes the best decision is to wait.

24Can I ask for a very natural result?

Yes, and that should be said clearly. Natural-looking results usually require conservative planning, respect for proportions and patience to build improvement without changing expression or identity.

25Does care continue after the procedure?

Yes, especially when there is recovery, reaction risk or a plan with multiple sessions. Post-procedure guidance and a contact channel are part of care, not an extra favor.
