I do not believe aesthetic care should make everyone look the same.

#### I step away from

- Pressure to keep changing
- Treating natural variation as failure
- Intervention for intervention’s sake

> “The most elegant result is often the one that still feels completely like you.”

**01 · VALUE 01 · LISTENING**

### I listen before I recommend

Listening is not a polite step before the “real” consultation. It is the beginning of the reasoning.

- Give you time to describe the concern in your own words;
- Ask before suggesting a procedure;
- Recognise uncertainty instead of rushing past it;
- Understand what you do not want as clearly as what you do.

### In practice

You may request a particular laser, but the conversation may reveal that the real concern is sensitivity, pigmentation, texture or an overloaded routine. The first step may be preparation or another route.

> “A recommendation becomes personal only after the person has truly been heard.”

_Current visual context: Franciele Sofiati introducing her professional values_

### Available does not always mean appropriate

A device may be advanced and a treatment may be popular. Neither fact confirms that it belongs in your plan.

#### Concern

What are we actually trying to improve?

#### Person

What individual factors affect the plan?

#### Moment

Is the skin ready and is the timing responsible?

#### Recovery

Can the necessary aftercare be followed?

#### Expectation

Can the treatment realistically support the goal?

Would preparation, referral or waiting be better?

- Selected acne scars and other carefully assessed scars;
- Fine lines and wrinkles;
- Uneven texture;
- Selected signs of sun-related skin change.

### Timing is part of treatment safety

#### Recent tanning or sunburn

Additional pigment or inflammation can change risk.

#### Active irritation

Sensitive or injured skin may need time and supportive care.

#### Infection

Active bacterial, viral or fungal concerns should be assessed first.

#### An unexplained lesion

A changing, bleeding or irregular mark should not simply be treated as pigmentation.

#### Medication or health considerations

Some conditions, healing patterns or medicines may affect treatment choice.

#### Limited recovery time

An intensive procedure should not be forced into an unsuitable calendar.

4. Treat Select the handpiece and settings for the plan while monitoring comfort and response.

It may improve selected scar features, but it cannot restore injured skin exactly to its previous state.

The most accurate term is long-term hair reduction. Maintenance may be needed.

#### Can the laser be chosen from a photograph?

A photograph can begin a conversation, but it cannot confirm cause, depth, suitability or settings.

#### Botulinum Toxin — Platysma and Lower Face

##### What it is

May be considered for selected platysma bands or patterns of downward muscular pull. It is not equivalent to a surgical neck or lower-face lift.

##### What it may support

- Selected visible platysma bands;
- Specific patterns of downward muscular pull;
- A restrained lower-face planning approach.

##### Important to know

This treatment does not correct every cause of lower-face laxity or replace surgical lifting.

##### The conversation includes

- Muscular movement
- Facial balance
- Previous treatment response
- Natural expression
- Temporary effect and review

##### The treatment should not be sold as

- A permanent result
- A universal dose or map
- A surgical alternative in every case
- A promise of perfect symmetry

**08 · SCALP AND MICROINFUSION**

### The substance and diagnosis matter

#### MMP — Microinfusion of Medication into the Skin

##### What it is

A microneedle-assisted technique used to deliver a specifically selected substance into the skin or scalp. Its evidence, risks and expected effects depend on the substance, concentration, indication, technique and sterility.

##### What it may support

- Selected skin or scalp protocols when the substance and indication are appropriate.

##### Important to know

MMP should never be described as one standard treatment; the delivered substance is central to its evidence, risk and purpose.

#### Hair Mesotherapy

##### What it is

An intradermal scalp-injection technique whose suitability depends on the cause of hair loss and the substance proposed. It should not be described as a guaranteed hair-growth treatment.

##### What it may support

- Selected scalp treatment plans after appropriate assessment;
- Substance-specific protocols with realistic expectations.

##### Important to know

Hair loss has many causes. Diagnosis or dermatological evaluation may be needed before an injection-based plan is considered.

##### Hair loss may need investigation

Sudden, patchy, scarring, inflammatory or unexplained hair loss may require dermatological or medical evaluation before an aesthetic scalp protocol.

**09 · SUPERFICIAL MICROVESSELS**

### Selected vascular treatment after assessment

#### PEIM — Aesthetic Injectable Treatment for Microvessels

##### What it is

An injectable sclerotherapy approach used for selected superficial telangiectasias, commonly called spider veins. The sclerosant, compression advice and aftercare depend on vascular assessment and the vessels being treated.

##### What it may support

- Selected superficial spider veins after assessment;
- A planned vascular-aesthetic treatment sequence.

##### Important to know

Not every visible vessel is suitable for injection. Deeper venous disease may require another professional evaluation.

- Not every visible vessel is suitable for the same injection;
- Deeper venous concerns may need another evaluation;
- Pigmentation, inflammation, ulceration, thrombosis and allergic reactions are possible risks;
- Follow-up helps determine whether and when another session should be considered.

### The first photograph is not always the final result

#### Immediate effects

Some redness, swelling or temporary surface change may be visible first.

#### Early recovery

Peeling, bruising or treated pigment may temporarily change appearance.

#### Developing effect

Botulinum toxin and tissue-remodelling treatments need time before review.

#### Series-based care

Hair reduction, pigmentation and scar plans may involve several sessions.

#### Long-term response

Ageing, hormones, sun exposure and recurrence can influence maintenance.

#### Review point

Results should be assessed at an appropriate interval—not simply as soon as possible.

**05 · PHOTOGRAPHY INTEGRITY**

### A comparison should not create a false difference

#### A useful comparison keeps

- Similar lighting
- Similar angle and distance
- Similar expression and posture
- No beauty filters
- A clear treatment interval
- The same area visible

#### A misleading comparison may use

- Different shadows or exposure
- Different facial expression
- Makeup in only one image
- Retouching or smoothing
- Immediate swelling as a final result
- Unstated treatment combinations

#### Context belongs beside the image

When possible, result content should identify the treatment, number of sessions, timing and relevant limitations without revealing private patient information.

**06 · NATURAL-LOOKING RESULTS**

### Individuality should remain visible

Natural-looking care is not one fixed aesthetic. It means the degree of change respects the person’s anatomy, expression, preferences and sense of self.

#### Expression

Movement is considered rather than automatically treated as a problem.

#### Proportion

The face or body is assessed as a whole rather than as isolated flaws.

#### Restraint

More treatment is not assumed to create a better outcome.

#### Preference

A subtle change and a more visible change are not the same request.

#### Identity

The objective is not to copy another person’s appearance.

#### Time

Gradual change may feel more appropriate than an immediate transformation.

> “A refined result should still feel recognisably yours.”

**07 · IMPROVEMENT IS NOT ERASURE**

### Responsible language protects expectations

#### Care may help

- Soften selected lines
- Reduce suitable hair
- Improve selected pigment
- Refine texture
- Soften scar features
- Support mild tightening

#### Care should not promise

- No movement or pores
- Removal of every hair forever
- Permanent control of every pigment condition
- Perfectly smooth skin
- Complete scar disappearance
- A surgical result from a non-surgical treatment

> “The word “improve” is not weak language. It is often the most honest language.”

**08 · MAINTENANCE AND RECURRENCE**

### Some results need continuity

Aesthetic care does not stop time or remove every trigger. Results may evolve as the body, skin and lifestyle continue to change.

- Botulinum toxin is temporary;
- Hair may need maintenance, especially when hormonal influences continue;
- Melasma and some pigmentation can recur;
- Skin ageing and sun exposure continue;
- Home care can support—but not freeze—a result;
- Future treatment should be based on review rather than an automatic schedule.

**09 · WHEN A RESULT FEELS DIFFERENT FROM EXPECTED**

### Review before reacting

The first step is to compare the response with the expected timeline and treatment information. Some effects settle or develop gradually, while others need prompt assessment.

#### Too early to judge

The treatment may still be healing or developing.

#### Unexpected reaction

Pain, spreading redness, blistering or another unusual change needs contact.

#### Expectation mismatch

The original goal and treatment limits may need to be discussed again.

#### Asymmetry or uneven response

Anatomy, swelling and treatment response should be evaluated at the correct time.

#### Further treatment

Another session should not be assumed before the first response is reviewed.

#### No further treatment

Sometimes the most appropriate decision is to stop, wait or choose another route.

# Content and visual-direction analysis

## Executive diagnosis

The site has a clear and unusually responsible point of view: consultation before intervention, context before comparison, restraint before escalation, and aftercare as part of treatment. That is the brand. The strongest mockups should make this judgment-led approach visible rather than defaulting to generic luxury-clinic imagery.

The current copy is comprehensive, reassuring and ethically careful. Its main design challenge is volume and recurrence. Twenty-one routes repeatedly use ten-chapter structures, card collections, paired contrasts and cautionary statements. If every idea receives the same card treatment, equally weighted sections make the experience feel longer and flatter than the underlying content deserves. The redesign opportunity is not “more decoration”; it is sharper hierarchy, more evidence-specific imagery and more distinct page identities.

## What the content says the brand actually is

1. **A guide, not a seller.** The dominant verbs are understand, assess, explain, prepare, review and adapt. Imagery should show listening, observation and planning as often as procedures.
2. **Clinically careful but not hospital-cold.** The copy combines safety limits with warm human language. The visual world should combine precise framing, honest light and tactile natural materials.
3. **Anti-hype and anti-erasure.** Results are discussed as individual, time-dependent and contextual. Avoid glossy “perfect skin,” dramatic transformation tropes and visual language that suggests guaranteed outcomes.
4. **Continuity is a service.** Preparation, recovery, contact and review recur throughout the site. Timelines, care cards, annotated sequences and follow-up moments can make continuity tangible.
5. **Franciele’s judgment is the differentiator.** Technology and treatment names are tools. The professional’s evaluation is the connecting story. Portraits should therefore feel attentive and working, not merely posed.

## Content strengths to preserve

- Headings frequently work as memorable principles: “Care without punishment,” “Improvement is not erasure,” and “The image is only one layer.” These are strong editorial anchors.
- The copy answers anxious, real-world questions about timing, discomfort, suitability, recovery and uncertainty.
- Safety language is specific enough to be useful without turning the whole brand alarmist.
- The treatment content consistently explains limits, which creates credibility.
- Cross-links support exploratory visitors who know their concern but not the name of a procedure.
- Forms collect useful context and explain why information is requested.

## Content risks the mockups should solve

### 1. Repetition can make important distinctions disappear

Words such as context, clarity, individual, responsible, pressure, promise and understanding recur across many pages. They belong to the brand, but when every chapter uses the same visual weight, readers may remember the tone while missing the practical difference between pages.

**Mockup response:** give each major page family one unmistakable visual grammar. Skin can feel observational and editorial; Laser can feel precise and light-led; Results can resemble an evidence ledger; Care can use time and recovery sequencing; About can feel biographical and personal.

### 2. Ten-section pages produce uniform narrative pacing

The pages contain good material, but ten similarly sized chapters can feel like ten equal stops. Visitors need a visible distinction between the core answer, supporting explanation, evidence, safety limits and next action.

**Mockup response:** design three pacing levels: one dominant opening proposition, two or three expanded teaching chapters, and compact supporting chapters. Use image-led pauses or concise diagrams between dense text groups.

### 3. The visual evidence is less specific than the written evidence

The copy is precise about assessment, technologies, treatment intervals and photography integrity, while much of the current imagery is portrait or interior photography. That creates a gap between what the site says and what it proves visually.

**Mockup response:** add procedure-specific but restrained evidence: consultation notes, device handpieces, protective preparation, standardized photography setup, treatment-room details and aftercare materials. Never show invasive detail merely for drama.

### 4. Negative constructions dominate some chapters

“Not,” “without,” “cannot,” “should not” and “do not” are valuable for setting safe limits, but too many consecutive warnings can make the experience feel defensive.

**Mockup response:** pair boundaries with a positive visual route. A caution card can sit beside “what we do instead,” a preparation checklist, a review path or a calm human moment.

### 5. Conversion moments are repeated but not differentiated

“Request a consultation,” “Contact the clinic” and similar actions appear often. The actions are appropriate, but visitors may not understand when a simple message is enough and when a consultation is the better next step.

**Mockup response:** use a consistent two-route decision component: **I have a practical question** versus **I want an individual assessment**. Show response expectations, information needed and urgency limits directly in the component.

### 6. Trust is stated more often than demonstrated

Credentials, responsible language and patient words are present, but they can be integrated into richer proof.

**Mockup response:** create an evidence strip with credentials, years/areas of practice if supportable, clinic location, photography standards, aftercare access and sourced patient feedback. Only include verifiable facts; do not invent numbers.

## A coherent photography shot list

One disciplined shoot can supply most of the authentic assets needed for the mockups without repeating the same portrait everywhere:

1. **Consultation conversation:** wide, medium and detail crops; Franciele listening, explaining and taking notes; patient identity optional or kept outside frame.
2. **Working portraits:** three distinct moods—welcoming, analytical and reflective—with natural skin texture and consistent colour treatment.
3. **Clinic architecture:** reception, treatment room, consultation surface, doorway and quiet material details; level architectural lines and both horizontal/vertical crops.
4. **Assessment details:** non-diagnostic observation, note-taking, calendar/timing discussion and preparation explanation.
5. **Technology families:** accurate handpieces, protective preparation and device controls photographed consistently; no simulated treatment effect.
6. **Standardized result photography:** neutral background, fixed light, camera position, distance marker and metadata card.
7. **Aftercare continuity:** written guidance, sunscreen, clean towel, calendar, phone follow-up and review conversation.
8. **Editorial skin/routine still life:** neutral objects and diverse natural skin detail, with no brand sponsorship implied.

Shoot every essential setup in landscape, portrait and close-detail crops. Reserve clean negative space on both left and right so the same authentic material can support responsive editorial layouts without aggressive cropping.

# Guardrails for image creation

- Do not fabricate before-and-after results, testimonials, credentials or clinical settings and present them as real.
- Preserve natural skin texture and individual features; avoid beauty-filter aesthetics.
- Obtain specific consent for identifiable patients, treatment moments and result photography.
- Keep equipment accurate and secondary to professional judgment.
- Do not use imagery that implies one technology is suitable for everyone.
- Pair safety copy with clear action, not alarming imagery.
- Maintain consistent lighting, angle, crop and timing metadata for any real comparison.
- Write useful alt text during production and treat decorative textures as decorative.
